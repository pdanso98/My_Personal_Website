Measuring how geography, infrastructure, and policy shape access to health care.

I am a PhD student in the Department of Geography at the University of Florida, developing spatial models of healthcare and telehealth accessibility. My research asks a simple question with complicated answers: who can actually reach care, and why?

Research

Three threads, one map

01 · telehealth

Telehealth and regulatory geography

Virtual care removes the drive, but not every barrier. I study how regulation and broadband availability decide where telehealth actually works, and how to build those real-world constraints into spatial accessibility models.

02 · methods

Stress-testing accessibility models

Accessibility estimates are only as good as the assumptions behind them. My published work applies global sensitivity analysis and system dynamics to find which inputs really move the results, across both space and time.

03 · cities

Neighborhood change

My master's research traced studentification around Texas Tech University, combining census, property, and field data to show how student housing reshapes the demographics and fabric of nearby neighborhoods.

Dissertation, in brief

My dissertation advances accessibility modeling so that estimates of healthcare and telehealth access reflect the regulatory, infrastructural, and operational realities of how care is delivered, with telepsychology as one of several demonstration cases.
